About the Project

Formalizing the Hero’s Journey

In 1949, Joseph Campbell published The Hero with a Thousand Faces, a work of comparative mythology arguing that heroic myths across cultures, from Sumerian descents to Promethean cycles, betray a single generative sequence he termed the monomyth: a departure, an initiation, and a return, each threshold marking a transformation in the hero's ontological status.

Campbell's framework was shaped decisively by Carl Jung's theory of archetypes, which posited recurring mythological figures as expressions of a collective unconscious transcending individual experience. Campbell gave this psychological machinery a narratological armature, mapping psychic individuation onto the plane of story, a map contemporary filmmakers and game designers still reach for, whether consciously or through convergent intuition.

What makes the monomyth compelling as an object of formal study is the tension between its claim to universality and the irreducible particularity of any given narrative. Every story read through Campbell's stages also resists that reading at determinate points, producing divergences and inversions that constitute the most analytically productive sites of inquiry. It is this generative friction, the gap between the structural template and its concrete realization, that invites a rigorous, systematic treatment capable of capturing both the pattern and its deformations.

  1. 01 Universal narrative grammar — Beneath the surface variety of world mythologies lies a single recurring pattern, the monomyth: a three-act arc of departure, initiation, and return.
  2. 02 Shared vocabulary — Archetypes populate the journey, two worlds define its spatial logic, a threshold divides them, trials test the hero's transformation, and a boon rewards the return.
  3. 03 Variation as structure — Each narrative reshapes, inverts, or omits stages: the symbolic vocabulary changes across cultures and media, but the underlying transformative pattern persists through its own deformations.
“The hero ventures forth from the world of common day into a region of supernatural wonder: fabulous forces are there encountered and a decisive victory is won: the hero comes back from this mysterious adventure with the power to bestow boons on his fellow man.” — Joseph Campbell, The Hero with a Thousand Faces (1949)

The Ontology

A Vocabulary for Myth

The ontology defines a reusable vocabulary at monomyth: that captures the deep structure of Campbell's monomyth. Expressed in OWL and serialized as Turtle, it models the seventeen stages across three acts, the archetypal roles that populate the journey, and the evaluative apparatus needed to assess how faithfully (or how creatively) any given narrative instantiates the pattern. Every analytical judgment, from the quality of a stage's manifestation to the rationale behind a departure from the template, is represented as a first-class, queryable entity rather than a background assumption.

The central modeling pattern is the MonomythExpression, a reification of the interpretive act itself: the structured reading of a particular narrative work through Campbell's lens. Each expression anchors a constellation of StageRealizations, the concrete manifestations of abstract stages within that narrative. A realization carries a prose description of the narrative moment it captures, a position in the story's own sequence (which may diverge from the canonical ordering), and a FitQuality assessment on a six-point scale ranging from Perfect Fit through Strong, Moderate, and Weak to Absent and Inverted, each backed by a numeric score and an optional analytical note.

The treatment of characters draws on Christopher Vogler's The Writer's Journey, a screenwriting manual that deliberately translates Campbell's mythological archetypes into a practical dramaturgy of recurring roles. The ontology formalizes it through the embodiesArchetype property, linking characters to archetypal roles while acknowledging their productive instability: a figure who operates as Mentor in one stage may reveal itself as Shadow in another, and the graph preserves this fluidity as structured, traversable data.

Where the alignment between a narrative and the monomyth template is imperfect, the ontology does not simply record the gap: it models the nature of the departure through three orthogonal divergence classes, all subclasses of CREON's ProductDivergence. A NarrativeDivergence captures what happens when a stage is absent, subverted, compressed, or redistributed across multiple narrative moments. A SequentialDivergence marks when a stage occurs and whether the narrative has displaced it from its canonical position for dramatic or thematic effect. A SemioticDivergence traces how the stage is expressed, underlying the shift in sign systems (from sacred to secular, from mythic to technological, from external threat to internal state) through which the same structural function is realized in a different cultural or medial vocabulary. Each divergence carries a prose rationale documenting the artistic purpose behind the departure, making explicit that imperfect fit is itself an object of analysis, often the most revealing one.

Formalizing a hermeneutic tradition into computational structure risks flattening the very interpretive richness it seeks to preserve. This ontology addresses this tension by treating deviation as constitutive: conformity and subversion, presence and absence, canonical order and displacement are granted equal analytical weight. The deeper question it encodes is how a pattern largely unchanged for millennia has generated such radically diverse stories, and how each creative departure, every inversion or omission, is itself a meaningful act.

monomyth:NarrativeWork
Narrative Work

Monomyth Expression
A specific realization of the monomyth structure within a particular narrative work. One work may contain multiple expressions for different characters.
monomyth:Character
Character
A character within a specific narrative work, linked to archetypes and involved in stage realizations across the journey.
monomyth:Archetype
Archetype
A recurring character role based on Vogler's adaptation of Campbell: Hero, Mentor, Herald, Threshold Guardian, Shadow, Shapeshifter, Trickster, and Ally.
monomyth:StageRealization
Stage Realization
The key modeling pattern: how an abstract stage concretely manifests in a narrative, with fit quality, prose description, and ordering.
monomyth:FitQuality
Fit Quality
A six-point scale from InvertedFit through AbsentFit, WeakFit, ModerateFit, StrongFit, to PerfectFit for assessing stage mappings.
monomyth:NarrativeDivergence
Narrative Divergence
A creative departure from the expected monomyth pattern, formalizing the insight that imperfect alignments are not failures of fit but potential acts of deliberate artistic choice.
monomyth:SequentialDivergence
Sequential Divergence
A structural departure from the expected ordering of monomyth stages, occurring when a narrative realizes a stage earlier or later than its canonical position.
monomyth:SemioticDivergence
Semiotic Divergence
A divergence in the sign system through which a stage is expressed, where the cultural or figurative vehicle changes while structural function is preserved.

The Matrix

Movie Knowledge Graph
The Wachowskis, 1999

Released on the cusp of the new millennium, The Matrix captures an era of ontological anxiety fueled by the digital frontier and the suspicion that reality is a construct. The film aligns precisely with the hero’s transition from a sterile mundane world into a hidden reality. Neo’s guides provide a catalyst, but the aid he receives undergoes a crucial epistemic shift: his help is privileged information revealing the world as a simulation, reflecting the late-90s view of hackers as modern shamans.
The strongest alignment lies in the rigorous testing Neo undergoes once extracted. This mirrors the classic road of tests perfectly; the hero must unlearn physical limitations to master a new environment. These trials emphasize the sovereignty of the mind over the body, treating systemic mastery as a spiritual evolution.
The work departs in its depiction of temptation. The distraction is not a person but the seductive comfort of systemic ignorance. Through Cypher, the narrative weighs painful truth against a corporate simulation, reflecting the new millennium's fears of totalizing consumer culture. The final transcendence is re-signified through a digital apotheosis, where divinity is the total manipulation of code.
The journey concludes by subverting the homecoming. Neo cannot return to a society that was always a lie. Instead, he masters the system to offer liberation, shifting the circular monomyth toward an evolutionary trajectory. Once the veil is lifted, there is no "normal" home — only the mission to dismantle the artificial.

The Lion King

Animated Movie Knowledge Graph
Roger Allers & Rob Minkoff, 1994

Released during the height of the Disney Renaissance, The Lion King closely follows Joseph Campbell’s monomyth structure, especially in Simba’s encounter with Mufasa’s ghost. The scene functions as a classic call to transformation, forcing Simba to abandon the false identity he built in exile and reclaim his role as king. At the same time, the film adapts Campbell’s framework through an African ancestral cosmology, where the dead remain present in nature, while also reflecting the 1990s cultural focus on paternal validation and emotional healing.

What makes the film distinctive is its emphasis on guilt and avoidance rather than wonder. Simba’s refusal to return happens early because he believes he caused his father’s death, turning exile into a psychological escape rather than a heroic pause. This reflects the decade’s interest in trauma and the “inner child.” The eventual revelation of Scar’s betrayal becomes the true boon of the journey, one that can only occur publicly before the community. The film also reframes temptation through the philosophy of Hakuna Matata, presenting disengagement and irresponsibility as an appealing alternative identity. In this version of the hero’s journey, comfort itself becomes the greatest obstacle to growth.

The Call of the Wild

Novel Knowledge Graph
Jack London, 1903

Published in 1903 during the rise of American naturalism, The Call of the Wild reflects a worldview shaped by Darwinian thought, where survival and environment matter more than moral choice. By making Buck, a dog, the protagonist, Jack London removes human exceptionalism and creates a version of the hero’s journey that follows Campbell’s framework closely at first but ultimately rejects its final stage. Buck’s removal from domestic life, harsh education in the Klondike, and gradual transformation through violence and survival mirror the classic road of trials. Each new owner strips away another layer of civilization, while the brutal lessons of survival reflect the era’s fascination with Social Darwinism.

The emotional center of the novella lies in Buck’s relationship with John Thornton, whose love becomes both a source of belonging and the final temptation preventing Buck from fully entering the wild. Buck’s pull between campfire and wilderness reflects contemporary anxieties about primitivism and atavism, the belief that civilization hides a more ancient instinctive self. Unlike Campbell’s circular monomyth, however, Buck never returns home. After Thornton’s death, the journey becomes irreversible, ending not with restoration but with permanent transformation. London suggests that true freedom comes from completing the crossing into the wild rather than returning from it, turning the traditional heroic return into an illusion.

The Rostam and Haft-Khan

Epic Poem Knowledge Graph
Ferdowsi, 1010

Ferdowsi’s Shahnameh, written in the 10th century, is one of the most important works of Persian cultural preservation. Composed after the Islamic conquest of Iran, the epic sought to revive the Persian language and preserve pre-Islamic history at a time when Arabic dominated intellectual and political life. By minimizing Arabic vocabulary, Ferdowsi created a literary foundation that still remains readable to modern Persian speakers today. Beyond language, the Shahnameh became a cultural memory of Iran, presenting heroic figures such as Rostam as symbols of a shared national identity. In monomythic terms, the epic itself functions as an Ultimate Boon, restoring cultural vision and continuity to a threatened society.

The Haft Khan follows Rostam as he journeys through seven dangerous trials to rescue King Kay Kavus, whose arrogance led him into disaster. Unlike many Western hero myths, authority in the story is reversed: the king is weak and helpless, while the hero must restore the throne rather than seek approval from it. Rostam’s horse, Rakhsh, acts as an intelligent ally rather than a passive tool, emphasizing cooperation instead of solitary heroism. The narrative also treats temptation differently from Western traditions. In the fourth labor, a sorceress disguises herself as beauty, but the illusion is destroyed when Rostam invokes God’s name, revealing evil as something supernatural rather than psychological. The journey ends with the blood of the White Demon, a literal elixir that restores the king’s sight and symbolically renews the political and moral order of Iran.

The Secret Life of Walter Mitty

Movie Knowledge Graph
Ben Stiller, 2013

Ben Stiller’s The Secret Life of Walter Mitty transforms the hero’s journey into an intensely internalized quest set within the modern corporate world. Walter, a negative assets manager at Life magazine, spends his life cataloging the adventures of others while escaping into elaborate fantasies of heroism. When the crucial “Negative #25” from photographer Sean O’Connell goes missing during the magazine’s transition from print to digital, Walter is forced to leave his routine behind and travel across the world in search of the image. The story reflects contemporary anxieties about disappearing physical history and the isolation of modern work culture.

The film reshapes the traditional monomyth through psychological rather than physical conflict. Walter’s Refusal of the Call appears as maladaptive daydreaming, where fantasy becomes a barrier protecting him from real risk and emotional vulnerability. The narrative also introduces a “Premature Return,” when Walter is forced back to New York after failure, revealing that the modern hero must first confront insecurity and defeat before transformation becomes possible. Sean O’Connell functions as a secular father- figure who recognizes Walter’s hidden value rather than judging him. The film’s Ultimate Boon is divided between the missing negative itself and Walter’s deeper realization of self-worth. By the end, Walter achieves mastery not over mythical monsters but over the social anxieties and routines that once controlled his life, discovering meaning within ordinary existence rather than escaping from it.

Batman: Year One

Comic Knowledge Graph
Frank Miller, 1987

Batman: Year One (1987) reimagines the superhero origin as Neo-Noir crime fiction, secularizing Campbell’s monomyth through urban realism. Cosmic realms and magical talismans are replaced by Gotham’s institutional decay: Supernatural Aid becomes Bruce Wayne’s internalized bat-symbol, triggered by the animal crashing through his window, while the Belly of the Whale is localized in firebombed tenements and savage beatings by corrupt police. The heroic journey is thus translated into the violent language of systemic corruption rather than epic fantasy.
Structurally, the narrative diverges sharply from Campbell through Batman’s agency. Bruce is never a reluctant hero refusing the call; his “refusal” is instead a premature, failed attempt at vigilantism before refining his methods. Likewise, he does not negotiate with Threshold Guardians — he invades Gotham’s elite and terrorizes them directly. Most crucially, Batman bypasses the Temptress stage entirely through semiotic dehumanization: Bruce fractures his identity into an incorruptible symbol of fear, a sign with no negotiable interiority and therefore no susceptibility to earthly seduction.
To preserve human vulnerability, the story displaces the Temptress function onto co-protagonist Jim Gordon, whose affair with Sarah Essen threatens his moral crusade. Their parallel journeys converge when Batman saves Gordon’s infant son, making the Ultimate Boon not a grail or elixir but a social contract: a clandestine alliance between two damaged men.
Because it is an origin story, the arc deliberately omits Campbell’s Return; there is no restored ordinary world, only the forging of identities and partnership.

Oedipus Rex & Oedipus at Colonus

Play Knowledge Graph
Sophocles, 425/401 BCE

The myth of Oedipus challenges the traditional structure of the monomyth because the hero is also the shadow he hunts, turning the journey into a tragic cycle driven by fate. Rather than escaping destiny, every action Oedipus takes leads him closer to fulfilling Apollo’s prophecy. The journey begins with the Delphic Oracle, which functions as a displaced form of Supernatural Aid by revealing the prophecy before the quest truly begins. Oedipus’ flight from Corinth becomes an inverted Refusal of the Call: he leaves not out of fear, but from a sincere attempt to protect the people he believes are his parents. Ironically, this decision becomes the mechanism that fulfills the prophecy itself.

The initiation phase transforms key monomythic stages into tragic reversals. The Atonement with the Father becomes literal patricide at the crossroads, where Oedipus destroys the father figure rather than reconciling with him. Jocasta similarly reworks the Woman as Temptress archetype, not as a corrupting force but as someone who urges Oedipus to abandon his destructive search for truth. The story’s Ultimate Boon arrives through self-blinding, an inward vision gained through suffering rather than enlightenment. Unlike Campbell’s circular return, Oedipus never reintegrates into society. In Oedipus at Colonus, his final transformation comes only through death, where he becomes a sacred protector figure, turning from a cursed outcast into a divine presence.

SABLE, fABLE

Album Knowledge Graph
Bon Iver, 2025

SABLE, fABLE (2025) reimagines Campbell’s monomyth through radical psychological intimacy rather than mythic scale. The heroic journey is compressed into an isolated autobiography centered on only three figures: the hero, his mirrored self, and his romantic partner. Cosmic realms and supernatural forces vanish entirely, replaced by the secular language of therapy and trauma recovery.
Intrusive thoughts become the Herald, fear of change functions as the Refusal, and the Belly of the Whale is relocated to the suffocating guilt of a self-destructive “violent spree” in Key West. Mythic conflict is thus internalized into psychological regression. Vernon’s monsters are his impulses toward familiar isolation and melancholy, while the Road of Trials becomes the exhausting emotional labor of vulnerability: allowing oneself to be seen in pain.
Because the wound is self-inflicted, the Ultimate Boon cannot be a grail or throne, but emotional capacity itself — the ability to “give me your worry” after years of being consumed by personal anguish. Mastery of the “two worlds” becomes both geographic and mental equilibrium, balancing the snowy isolation of SABLE, with the communal warmth of fABLE.
The album further diverges from Campbell by secularizing divine love. Rather than mystical transcendence, salvation comes through a partner whose defining heroic act is simply remaining present. Apotheosis is not godhood but the earthly relief of a shared “walk home,” while the Rescue from Without arrives through the promise, “I won’t move.” Love becomes not a cosmic reward, but a human anchor pulling the hero from spiraling introspection toward a life with “no more shame.”

Lady Bird

Movie Knowledge Graph
Greta Gerwig, 2017

Greta Gerwig’s Lady Bird functions as a modern, secular subversion of the monomyth, grounding Campbell’s heroic archetypes in the socioeconomic realities of early-2000s California and in the complexity of mother-daughter dynamics. Unlike the traditional hero guided by destiny, Christine McPherson’s journey begins with an inward call shaped by ambition and by her rejection of Sacramento, the cultural “desert” she longs to escape. The narrative develops as a double journey: a physical movement toward the East Coast and an internal evolution from the self-invented “Lady Bird” persona back to a more mature Christine, capable of gratitude and emotional growth.

The film replaces supernatural forces with institutional mechanisms: scholarships and financial aid become the bureaucratic equivalent of Supernatural Aid, the “magic” that allows social mobility. Christine also inverts the Refusal of the Call, actively abandoning her ordinary identity through rebellion and conflict with her mother, Marion. The parental roles are radically reworked: Larry embodies the Goddess archetype through unconditional support, while Marion becomes a version of the Woman as Temptress, discouraging Christine by emphasizing limits, fear, and economic reality. The Atonement occurs only after Christine’s move to New York, where her mother’s hidden letters reveal the care beneath her harshness. This reconciliation leads Christine to abandon the “Lady Bird” identity and reclaim her real name. The journey ends with a Mastery of Two Worlds, as she learns to balance New York aspirations with Sacramento roots, achieving maturity through gratitude and self-recognition.

Aeneid

Epic poem Knowledge Graph
Virgil, 19 BC

Virgil’s Aeneid occupies a singular place in the monomyth tradition because it consciously functions as the founding myth of an existing political order, reshaping Campbell’s sequence while preserving its deep grammar. The poem opens inside the dissolving darkness that should arrive later, with the storm at sea presented as condition rather than climax, while the hero’s journey is reconstructed etrospectively through Aeneas’s own narration. The summons appears only when Mercury intervenes at Carthage, after the temptress episode has nearly concluded the story. This Homeric in medias res structure disrupts the sequence without erasing it.
The strongest archetypal alignment occurs with Anchises in the Elysian fields, where paternal authority defines the hero’s identity with unmatched formal completeness. Yet the encounter is transformed tonally: instead of a threatening patriarch, Anchises weeps, embraces, and instructs. Authority becomes revelation, reflecting Augustan ideals of filial pietas and Stoic-Platonic cosmology.
Virgil’s greatest deviations center on feminine archetypes. The goddess figure is dispersed across Venus, Creusa, and Lavinia, whose burning hair marks her as a dynastic cipher rather than an agent of personal transformation. Dido embodies the temptress archetype with unusual clarity, but in displaced order, which gives her tragedy overwhelming dramatic force.
Most striking is the ending’s inversion. The hero, who should transcend fear and attachment, instead kills Turnus in fury, the final image tracking his soul fleeing beneath the shades. After Actium and the proscriptions, the cost of foundation could not be sublimated into peace, and Virgil refuses the consoling closure the monomyth traditionally promises.

The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time

Video game Knowledge Graph
Shigeru Miyamoto, Eiji Aonuma, 1998
Ocarina of Time (1998) embeds Campbell’s monomyth within an interactive medium that typically resists linearity. While videogames offer player freedom, Zelda disciplines its world to maintain a mythic register. The Departure is textbook: Link, defined by his lack of a fairy, receives a summons and companion, crossing into the unknown. The "Belly of the Whale" provides the work's strongest alignment: a seven-year sacred sleep that annihilates childhood. By temporarily revoking interactivity, the game forces a literal death-and-rebirth that the player cannot refuse.

The primary structural shift is sequential. Engulfment occurs mid-trials, bifurcating the journey into innocent childhood preparation and adult confrontation with a ruin. This temporal architecture reflects 1990s Japanese anxieties about "stolen time" and "lost childhood."

The Return act is where adherence to the monomyth frays most productively. The father archetype becomes a gentle revelation of orphanhood; the return threshold is a temporal membrane whose crossing erases the public evidence of everything Link achieved. The final image mirrors the opening, but the symmetry is tragic. The hero's mastery is entirely private, and his "Freedom to Live" is the loneliness of being the only person who remembers a future that no longer exists.

Orlando Furioso

Epic Poem Knowledge Graph
Lodovico Ariosto, 1532

Orlando Furioso (1532) is the definitive Renaissance chivalric epic, a genre naturally aligned with Campbell’s monomyth through its heroic quests, enchanted worlds, and providential destinies. Among its many intertwined narratives, the journey of Ruggiero forms the poem’s central structural and moral arc. As the Saracen ancestor of the Este dynasty, his transformation from pagan warrior to Christian founder closely follows the Hero’s Journey while being reshaped by Ariosto’s technique of entrelacement.

In Ruggiero’s journey, the monomyth appears as a dispersed and cyclical structure rather than a linear progression. Ariosto constantly interrupts Ruggiero’s journey, causing several stages to recur across the poem. The Belly of the Whale reappears through repeated enchantments and imprisonments within Atlante’s illusory spaces, turning symbolic death into a recurring condition. This cyclical entrapment makes rescue and recovery structural motifs, with Bradamante and Melissa repeatedly restoring the hero to his path.

Major divergences emerge in the Initiation act. Atlante transforms Atonement with the Father into a repeated struggle against protective paternal authority, while Bradamante redefines the Goddess archetype as both beloved and active rescuer. In the Return, Apotheosis becomes literal Christian baptism, and the Ultimate Boon is achieved in the form of the founding of the Este dynasty. Ruggiero ultimately masters both Saracen and Christian worlds, though his success remains shadowed by the prophecy of his early death.

Conclusion

The Pattern Persists

The monomyth functions not as a prescriptive template, but as a structural baseline across diverse narrative histories. While certain works align strictly with this framework, others derive their aesthetic or narrative efficacy through deliberate divergence from the expected archetype.

Formalizing Campbell’s framework in OWL bridges literary analysis and computational semantics, establishing a model for the computable humanities. This approach preserves narrative complexity while enabling systematic, scalable comparison. Consequently, the resulting knowledge graphs act as analytical instruments, formalizing how specific cultural and authorial contexts navigate the underlying mechanics of departure, ordeal, and return.

However, the instantiation of these knowledge graphs also highlighted the inherent subjectivity of structural mapping. During the peer-review phase, it became evident that aligning a narrative to a formalized framework is not a purely mechanical extraction. Because researchers evaluate source material through differing critical and analytical paradigms, defining a structural fit constitutes an active, interpretive appraisal of the text.

This interpretive variance manifested directly in the execution of the ontology’s technical architecture. For example, to formalize a missing stage, some researchers consistently paired a monomyth:AbsentFit with an explicit monomyth:NarrativeDivergence, while others treated the AbsentFit itself as a functionally complete ontological statement. Similarly, when a singular thematic subversion impacted multiple stages, certain modelers mapped a single divergence node to several stages, whereas others instantiated distinct divergences or omitted redundant links. Ultimately, these methodological deviations underscore a fundamental characteristic of semantic modeling in the humanities: while an ontology provides a rigorous, standardized schema, the resulting semantic data inherently encodes the critical choices of the human annotator.

Future implementations: formalizing Meta-Convergence

Although our Monomyth ontology doesn't explicitly reuse creon:MetaConvergence, it inherently executes its core mechanism. CreOn defines meta-convergence as the critical appraisal of a creative product's divergence against expected background knowledge to make it functionally or aesthetically effective. Our framework performs this function by using Campbell's monomyth as the structural baseline, while applying specific divergence classes (monomyth:NarrativeDivergence, monomyth:SequentialDivergence, and monomyth:SemioticDivergence) to evaluate creative departures. Both models view these deviations as deliberate, meaningful choices rather than simple errors. To formally implement this connection in the future, we could map our monomyth:FitQuality scores and monomyth:divergenceRationale descriptions directly to the creon:MetaConvergence class.

Expanding the paradigm: beyond Campbell's Monomyth

The ultimate extensibility of this project lies in the potential to expand our foundational schema to transcend the structural limitations of Joseph Campbell's original model. By integrating frameworks like Maureen Murdock's The Heroine's Journey and Kim Hudson's The Virgin's Promise into our ontology.ttl, we could broaden the graph's analytical power to capture deeply internal, relational, and socially disruptive narrative arcs. While Campbell's monomyth maps an outward quest of conquest into the unknown, encoding these alternative frameworks would allow us to query entirely different narrative. Murdock provides a potential "feminine counter-arc" that tracks inward descent to integrate split psychic polarities, while Hudson's model captures transformation born from radical self-expression against a conformist community. Ultimately, by formalizing these alternative structures as linked data in the future, we could empower the ontology to successfully process genres that the traditional monomyth struggles with, proving that narratives prioritizing authenticity and inner balance are just as structurally rigorous as tales of external triumph.

Potential research: querying the Monomyth

By populating the knowledge graphs with diverse narrative in the future, would enable us to formulate complex research questions that query divergences across multiple cultural axes.

We could run SPARQL queries to discover, for instance, how the monomyth:SemioticDivergence shifts from literal magic to psychological trauma across centuries of storytelling, or whether authors of different genders systematically invert specific archetypes. Specifically, the structured nature of this dataset would allow researchers to track narrative shifts over time (mapping historical trends and the evolution of specific divergences), across space (contrasting Western and Eastern structural traditions), through distinct media (comparing structural constraints in literature, film, comics, and music), and across gender (analyzing how the gender of the author or protagonist dictates specific divergences).

Ultimately, this framework has the potential to transition the Monomyth from a static literary theory into a dynamic, quantitative research tool, ready to reveal exactly how modern culture bends ancient mythologies to reflect its own realities.

Scaling the graph: LLM-Assisted modeling

To rapidly scale the population of our ontology without sacrificing structural integrity, we employed an LLM-assisted knowledge grounding technique using Claude and Gemini. By supplying our baseline OWL ontology alongside a "gold-standard" exemplar (The Matrix), we created a short prompt to draft new narrative knowledge graphs.

The results provided a fascinating case study in LLM limitations. For newer or less famous works, analytical accuracy plummeted as the models confidently hallucinated plot points to force a fit into Campbell's framework.

Ultimately, LLM generation serves as a first-pass scaffolding, requiring human-in-the-loop revision. The AI can build the graph's syntactic skeleton, but identifying why an author deliberately subverted an archetype remains a human intellectual exercise.
