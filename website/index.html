About the Project

Formalizing the Hero's Journey

In 1949, Joseph Campbell published The Hero with a Thousand Faces, arguing that the world's myths share a single narrative skeleton—the monomyth. A hero ventures from the ordinary world into a region of supernatural wonder, wins a decisive victory, and returns transformed.

This project takes Campbell's framework and encodes it as a formal OWL ontology serialized in Turtle (TTL). Every stage, archetype, threshold, and symbolic element becomes a precisely defined class or property in a machine-readable vocabulary, enabling systematic comparison across thousands of years of storytelling.

By building knowledge graphs for individual narratives—from Gilgamesh to The Matrix—we can run SPARQL queries to discover which stories fit Campbell's template perfectly and where the pattern breaks down, revealing as much about narrative diversity as about narrative universals.

  1. 01 Study Campbell's framework — The 17 stages across three acts, the archetypal roles, and the spatial logic of ordinary vs. special worlds.
  2. 02 Design the ontology — Model classes, properties, and relationships in OWL, with reified patterns for archetype assignment and fit quality.
  3. 03 Instantiate knowledge graphs — For each narrative, create stage realizations with prose descriptions, fit assessments, and Wikidata links.
  4. 04 Query and compare — Use SPARQL to analyze patterns across works, finding universals and divergences in the monomyth structure.

The Ontology

A Vocabulary for Myth

The ontology defines a reusable vocabulary at monomyth# that captures the deep structure of Campbell's monomyth. At its heart is the StageRealization pattern—the concrete manifestation of an abstract stage in a specific narrative—which enables meaningful cross-work comparison.

Characters are linked to archetypes through reified ArchetypeRoles that can shift across stages, acknowledging that a Mentor in Act I may become a Shadow by Act III. Each stage realization carries a FitQuality assessment on a six-point scale from Inverted to Perfect Fit, grounding literary analysis in structured, queryable data.

Core Classes
monomyth:NarrativeWork
Narrative Work
A story, novel, epic, film, or other narrative. Linked to external identifiers via owl:sameAs for Wikidata integration.
monomyth:MonomythInstance
Monomyth Instance
A specific hero's journey within a work. One work may contain multiple instances for different characters.
monomyth:StageRealization
Stage Realization
The key modeling pattern: how an abstract stage concretely manifests in a narrative, with fit quality and prose description.
monomyth:Character
Character
A character within a narrative, linked to archetypes through reified ArchetypeRole assignments that can shift across stages.
monomyth:Archetype
Archetype
A recurring character role: Hero, Mentor, Herald, Threshold Guardian, Shadow, Shapeshifter, Trickster, and more.
monomyth:FitQuality
Fit Quality
A six-point scale from Inverted (0) through Absent, Weak, Moderate, Strong, to Perfect Fit (5) for assessing stage mappings.
monomyth:NarrativeWorld
Narrative World
A distinct realm within the story—ordinary world, special world—modeling the spatial logic of the journey.
monomyth:Threshold
Threshold
A boundary between narrative worlds. Connected to worlds via connectsWorld, guarded by Threshold Guardian archetypes.
monomyth:SymbolicElement
Symbolic Element
Objects, places, or events carrying symbolic weight—red pills, white rabbits, golden fleeces—with annotated meanings.

Conclusion

The Pattern Persists

Across four millennia of storytelling—from Sumerian clay tablets to digital cinema—the monomyth endures not as a rigid template but as a gravitational center. Some narratives orbit it closely; others achieve their power precisely by breaking away. The ontology captures both: a Perfect Fit and an Inverted score are equally informative.

By formalizing Campbell's framework in OWL, we gain something neither pure literary criticism nor pure computer science could achieve alone: a computable humanities that respects the irreducible richness of narrative while enabling systematic comparison at scale. The knowledge graphs are not reductions of great stories—they are lenses, each one revealing how a particular culture, era, and author navigated the same deep structure of departure, ordeal, and return.
